01 Lead Intelligence
Growth Without Profit

Africa Grows. Africa Does Not Profit. Here Is What That Gap Means for a Lender.

Traffic growth of 8.8% in 2024. Net margins of 1.0%. Profit per passenger of $1.30 against a global average of $7.90. The paradox is not a problem to solve — it is a signal to read correctly.

Africa's aviation market is growing faster than any other region on earth. Passenger traffic expanded 8.8 percent in 2024 — against a global average of 6.6 percent. Cargo volumes rose 16.6 percent. Projected demand growth for 2026 is 6.0 percent, against a global forecast of 4.4 percent. Load factors currently sit at 74.1 percent — ten points below the global average — not because passengers are absent, but because there is not enough aircraft capacity to carry them. These are not conditions that warn a capital allocator away. They are conditions that reward one who enters with the right information.

And yet African carriers are projected to earn net margins of 1.0 to 1.1 percent in 2025 and 2026 — the lowest of any aviation region worldwide. Profit per passenger is approximately USD 1.30, against a global average of USD 7.90 and Gulf carriers earning USD 20 or more per seat. The entire African airline industry is projected to generate combined net profit of USD 400 million across both years — roughly what one mid-sized European carrier earns in a strong year alone.

"The margin problem is not a revenue problem. It is a cost structure problem — and cost structures can be addressed by financing architecture. That is the opportunity."

The Cost Structure: What Is Eating the Margin

African airlines pay fuel costs approximately 17 percent above the global average. Taxes and airport charges run 12 to 15 percent higher. Air navigation charges carry a further 10 percent premium. MRO, insurance, and capital costs add another 6 to 10 percent. Total unit costs for African carriers average 140 US cents per available tonne-kilometre — nearly double the global average of 80 cents. This is structural, not cyclical. It cannot be fixed by passenger growth. It can be materially improved by financing structures that reduce the cost of capital — which is exactly what properly structured leasing and credit facilities achieve.

The Blocked Funds Picture — Corrected

Nigeria — once the single largest source of blocked airline funds globally, with USD 850 million trapped at peak in June 2023 — is now entirely off the IATA watchlist. The backlog was cleared through CBN intervention and phased repatriation. As of October 2025, global blocked funds stand at USD 1.2 billion, with 93 percent concentrated in Africa and the Middle East. Algeria now leads at USD 307 million. Nigeria registers zero. The repatriation risk that defined this market three years ago has been resolved in Nigeria specifically. What remains is not the risk of repatriation failure — it is the risk of deploying without credit intelligence into a market where the legal framework has improved faster than the information infrastructure. That gap is where losses are made.

The Capital Translation

A lessor pricing African risk at a premium above European comparables is pricing opacity, not the asset. The aircraft operating a Lagos–Abuja route is the same aircraft as one on a London–Amsterdam sector. The documentation infrastructure around it, and the credit visibility of the operator flying it, is what varies. Andara Analytical View: every credit score issued to a Nigerian carrier, and every FleetWatch monitoring contract placed on a Nigerian-registered aircraft, removes a discount that exists only because no independent party was watching. The spread compression trade is available to capital allocators who replace opacity with intelligence before the rest of the market does.

Asset Intelligence — Green Africa Airways

The Lessor Event That Wasn't Monitored

In March 2025, Green Africa Airways experienced a total operational suspension triggered by what CEO Babawande Afolabi described publicly as a "sudden and unexpected development" with its lessor. All ATR 72 aircraft were grounded. Scheduled services were suspended across all routes through the Eid al-Fitr period. The lessor subsequently re-registered the aircraft off the Nigerian registry.

Events of this type develop over weeks and months through observable indicators: payment posture changes, maintenance interval slippage, documentation delays, regulatory correspondence patterns, and utilisation shifts. The intelligence gap is not that the crisis was unforeseeable — it is that no independent party was watching the signals.

Green Africa responded decisively. Within days of the announcement, the airline disclosed its first owned aircraft — ATR 72-500 (MSN 852, registration 5N-GAB), formerly with Aurigny and Korea's Hi Air — financed through a Naira-denominated debt facility with Access Bank. A second owned aircraft followed in February 2026: ATR 72-600 (MSN 1064, registration 5N-GAC), also Access Bank-financed. The pivot from full wet-lease dependency to asset ownership is a structurally positive credit development.

"The question is not what happened with Green Africa. It is: what would a monitoring party have known in advance — and what did the absence of monitoring cost the lessor?"

03 The Andara Signal — Nigeria

CTC Is Now Law. Enforcement Remains a Skill. Four Active Intelligence Variables Require Capital Attention.

Nigeria's Cape Town Convention compliance score reached 75.5 percent in October 2024, following the NCAA's issuance of Advisory Circular NCAA-AC-AWS001A and the formal signing of IDERA administrative rules. This moved Nigeria off the Aviation Working Group watchlist into the high-compliance category — with direct commercial consequences for lessors and lenders who had been effectively barred from the market for years.

The legal architecture is substantive. IDERA recordation gives a lessor, upon confirmed default, the right to request deregistration and export without a prior court order. The NCAA is mandated to process requests within five working days. The 2024 Federal High Court CTC Practice Directions require expedited handling. Nigeria's adoption of Article XI Alternative A means an airline in insolvency has a non-extendable 30-day window to cure or surrender. The first confirmed CTC-compliant repossession in Nigeria — Export Development Canada's recovery of a CRJ1000 from Arik Air (MSN 19037, reg. 5N-JEE) in November 2024 — establishes precedent.

What the Compliance Score Does Not Tell You

CTC compliance is a framework condition, not an execution guarantee. The gap between legal right and practical enforcement in Nigeria is well-documented by practitioners and is itself an intelligence variable. Nigerian courts have issued injunctions that temporarily blocked lessor self-help remedies, notwithstanding CTC supremacy — a pattern that is decreasing but not eliminated. The Nigerian Customs Service (NCS) creates an additional friction point: export of a repossessed aircraft requires customs clearance that operates on a separate timeline from NCAA deregistration. Ground handler liens can further complicate physical possession even where legal title is established. The first confirmed CTC-compliant repossession in Nigeria — EDC's recovery of a CRJ1000 from Arik Air (MSN 19037, reg. 5N-JEE) in November 2024 — was a historic milestone. It is also a single data point in a market of 12 scheduled carriers.

That buffer has a direct cost implication for lease rate factor and security deposit structure. The framework reduces execution risk. Intelligence eliminates it.

Governance Events as Credit Variables

For capital allocators, governance events at systemically important carriers must be treated as active credit variables, regardless of operational performance. The four signal events in this issue — an operational disruption at Green Africa, an active federal indictment at Air Peace, a structural financing risk at Bayelsa Air, and a compliance exposure across the private jet sector — are not separate stories. They are a single picture of what the absence of continuous, independent credit and asset intelligence produces.

Air Peace constitutes the most significant governance event in the Nigerian carrier market. As Nigeria's dominant carrier by fleet size, route coverage, and passenger volume, it represents the largest concentration of institutional capital exposure in domestic Nigerian aviation. A federal indictment filed by the US Department of Justice in November 2019 against Air Peace's founder, Chairman, and CEO remains active as of April 2026. Charges span bank fraud, money laundering, and — following a superseding indictment in October 2024 — obstruction of justice. The airline's Chief of Administration and Finance is co-charged. Air Peace and both individuals have denied all charges and assert the case does not affect operations. The matter remains before the US District Court, Northern District of Georgia; no trial date, acquittal, or dismissal has been confirmed as of publication. Under Andara's methodology, this constitutes a scored variable across Structural Viability, Payment Posture, and Regulatory Compliance dimensions — assessed as a counterparty risk signal, not a statement on operational safety. All matters referenced are publicly reported and treated strictly as credit-relevant variables within Andara's scoring framework.

04 The State Aviation Wave

Nine Nigerian States Now Own or Are Building Aviation Entities. No Standard Credit Framework Exists for Any of Them.

This is not an airline proliferation story. It is a credit methodology gap. Banks are financing these entities. Lessors are placing aircraft with them. The question of how to score them has not been answered.

The wave began with Ibom Air in 2019 — the first state-owned airline in Nigeria's history, wholly owned by Akwa Ibom State Government. Its performance has become the template every subsequent state is referencing. As of April 2026, Ibom Air operates nine aircraft — seven CRJ900s and two Airbus A220-300s — and is expanding internationally to Douala, São Tomé, and additional Ghana routes, with Victor Attah International Airport officially opening as an international hub in April 2026. The airline holds a firm order for ten A220-300s placed with Airbus in October 2021, with nine outstanding deliveries. Its A220 acquisitions have been financed by Nigerian domestic banks: the first A220 by Zenith Bank, the second from Carlyle Aviation financed by Fidelity Bank (majority) and Union Bank. Two additional CRJ900s (5N-CED, 5N-CEE) were financed via a shareholder loan from the state government. Ibom Air has IATA IOSA certification and is the benchmark. Every state airline entering this market is being measured against it — by banks, by lessors, and by passengers.

The second cohort followed quickly. Cally Air (Cross River State) operates two CRJ1000ERs leased from Aircraft Finance Germany under a ValueJet AOC. Enugu Air (Enugu State) received its own independent NCAA AOC on March 10, 2026, having been previously operated under XEJet's AOC — its fleet of two E170s, one E190, and one E195 will grow to a targeted 20 aircraft by year-end 2026. Bayelsa Air commissioned its first ATR 72-600 on November 26, 2025, financed by Premium Trust Bank, operated by Pioneer Airlines. Gateway Air (Ogun State) launched April 4, 2026 — commissioned personally by President Tinubu — with two CRJ900s (MSN 15312 reg. 5N-CFA and MSN 15285 reg. 5N-CEY), both former China Express Airlines aircraft, operated by ValueJet. Ogun State claims ownership. Air Peace simultaneously announced London Gatwick and Heathrow services to the new Gateway International Airport this summer.

Behind them: Caliphate Air (Sokoto State), ATL secured in 2025, AOC in process, evaluating E170/E190 fleet, summer 2026 launch targeted from Abuja. North-East Air Shuttle — six states (Adamawa, Bauchi, Borno, Gombe, Taraba, Yobe) pooling ₦30 billion at ₦5 billion per state, two aircraft targeted, no AOC filed yet. Ebonyi Air — three aircraft procured, January 2026 delivery targeted, type unconfirmed. Anambra, Abia, Edo, Delta, and Ekiti are at exploration or planning stage.

Andara Intelligence Flag — New Credit Category
State-owned Nigerian aviation entities are not commercial airline credits. They are sovereign-backed infrastructure commitments with aviation assets attached. The applicable credit questions are structurally different: Who holds the maintenance obligation — the state, the operating partner, or neither contractually? What is the succession risk when a new governor takes office? Is the financing secured against the aircraft, the state's FAAC allocation, or a state government guarantee? What happens to the asset when the operating partner's AOC — ValueJet, Pioneer, XEJet — lapses or changes? Ibom Air answers these questions because it was structured as an independent limited liability company with professional management and dedicated financing. Most of the second-cohort entities do not yet answer them clearly. Andara Analytical View: any bank or lessor financing a state aviation entity without step-in rights, maintenance escrow, and independent asset monitoring is not holding aircraft collateral. It is holding a political promise. The correct security framework for this asset class has not yet been established as market practice in Nigeria. 05 Capital in Motion

Who Is Financing Nigerian Aviation — and on What Basis

The Nigerian Aircraft Acquisition and Investment Summit (NAAIS 2026), held April 1–2 at the Federal Palace Hotel, Lagos, produced the most significant single week of aviation financing commitments in Nigerian history. The headline deal: a signed aircraft financing and leasing partnership between Aircraft Finance Germany (AFG) and Fidelity Bank — the first of its kind, designed to provide structured, sustainable financing solutions to Nigerian carriers through a combination of AFG's technical expertise and Fidelity's domestic banking strength. The deal was structured across four deal rooms: five Nigerian operators advanced ATR and Formidion aircraft discussions in Deal Room A; the CBN committed to sector-wide banking engagement and Embraer opened its Export Credit Agency window to Nigerian operators in Deal Room B; NAICOM committed to clarify the 10% local retention policy on aircraft acquisition in Deal Room C; FAAN and Afreximbank agreed on a bankable project pipeline and First Metro Infrastructure opened aerotropolis discussions at Lagos airport in Deal Room D. The second edition is already confirmed for April 1–2, 2027.

Andara Analytical View: NAAIS produced commitments and MOUs, not closed transactions — with the AFG/Fidelity partnership as the one signed deal. The intelligence question is what follows. The five unnamed operators who advanced discussions in Deal Room A represent the next layer of Nigerian carrier credit intelligence requirements. For every aircraft that moves from an ATR or Formidion discussion to a signed lease, a lender or lessor needs an independent credit assessment of the operator. That is Andara's function in this pipeline.

The broader Nigerian banking sector aviation exposure map has taken shape over the past 24 months. Fidelity Bank is the most active participant — financing Ibom Air's A220 acquisition, anchoring the AFG partnership, and publicly confirmed as supporting Air Peace. Zenith Bank financed Ibom Air's first A220 and is engaged in broader aviation finance. Access Bank financed Green Africa's two ATR 72s via Naira-denominated facilities. Union Bank co-financed Ibom Air's second A220 alongside Fidelity. Premium Trust Bank financed Bayelsa State's two ATR 72-600s. These five banks represent the core of domestic Nigerian aviation credit exposure. None of them has an independent credit intelligence infrastructure to support their portfolios. That is the gap Andara fills.

New entrant intelligence — Binani Air received its NCAA AOC for scheduled operations on March 17, 2026, becoming Nigeria's first female-founded airline. Owner: Senator Aishatu Dahiru Ahmed Binani (Adamawa State); CEO: Aminatu Dahiru Chiroma. Fleet: two Embraer E170s acquired from CIAF Leasing in Egypt — MSN 17000169 (reg. 5N-CCI, ex-EgyptAir Express, acquired May 2023) and MSN 170000170 (reg. 5N-CCJ, ex-Jasmin Airways) — both 18.8 years old, currently stored in Abuja. Also operating a Hawker 800XP (reg. 5N-CBM) on domestic charter. The E170s were acquired directly from CIAF Leasing. At 18.8 years of age, the maintenance economics of these assets in the Nigerian operating environment — and the adequacy of maintenance reserves being set aside — is an active intelligence question for any future financing structure against them.

For a bank carrying an aircraft as loan collateral: the value of that collateral is not static. It is a function of the aircraft's physical condition, its maintenance documentation currency, its regulatory standing, and the commercial posture of the operator flying it. All four of those variables change continuously. A bank that reviews collateral annually is not managing it. It is hoping. FleetWatch resolves this — monthly independent intelligence on aircraft condition, documentation, regulatory standing, and operational posture, sourced without reliance on what the operator chooses to disclose.
